Behaviorism

Background and Overview

Behaviorism dominated experimental psychology for several decades, and its influence can still be felt today. Early work in the field was conducted by John B. Watson at the beginning of the 20th century after important preliminary work by Ivan Pavlov and E. L. Thorndike and was popularised and radicalised in equal measure by B. F. Skinner in the 1950s. Behaviorists rejected the formerly popular introspectionist methods and instead focused on objectively observable, quantifiable events and behavior. Through its objective methods and experimentation, Behaviorism was also largely responsible for establishing psychology as a scientific discipline (Dumper et al., n.d.)

Classical Conditioning

Learning that occurs when a neutral, novel stimulus becomes associated with a natural, unconditioned stimulus that produces a behaviour / desired response.

Early work in the field was conducted by the Russian physiologist Ivan Pavlov (1849-1936). Pavlov conducted a series of experiments in which dogs were exposed to a sound immediately before receiving their food and recorded the amount of the dogs’ salivation. Initially the dogs salivated only when they saw or smelled the food, but after several pairings of the sound and the food, the dogs began to salivate as soon as they heard the sound without being exposed to food. The animals had learned to associate the sound with the food that followed. Pavlov had identified a fundamental associative learning process called classical conditioning (Stangor & Walina, 2014).

Source

  • 1: Before Conditioning: The food (Unconditioned Stimulus US) leads to the salivation of the dog (Unconditioned Response UR)
  • 2: Before Conditioning: The ringing of the bell (Neutral Stimulus NS) leads to no reaction of the dog (no conditioned response).
  • 3: During Conditioning: The ringing of the bell plus the simultaneously presentation of food leads to the salivation of the dog (UR).
  • 4: After Conditioning: The ringing of the bell (which is now a Conditioned Stimulus CS) leads to the salivation of the dog (Conditioned response) even though there is no food presented.

Related keywords and explanations: Extinction: if the CS is repeatedly presented alone -> the behaviour slowly decreases Spontaneous recovery: after a pause, when the CS is again presented alone, the behaviour may again occur and then again show extinction Stimulus Generalization: when a stimulus similar to an already-conditioned stimulus begins to produce the same response as the original stimulus (e.g. a dog being conditioned to being scratched before the food arrived, responded also with salivation when he was scrubbed) Stimulus Discrimination: when the organism learns to differentiate between the CS and other similar stimuli Second-order conditioning: a neutral stimulus becomes CS after being paired with a previously established CS Some stimuli-response pairs (like smell + food) are more easily conditioned (evolution)

In a classroom context we can think about the following example: when presented with a math flashcard showing the equation “2 + 4 = ?” the learner replies with the answer of “6.” The equation is the stimulus and the proper answer is the associated response. The key elements are the stimulus, the response, and the association between the two. Of primary concern is how the association between the stimulus and response is made, strengthened, and maintained. (Ertmer et al., 2018)

Operant Conditioning

Consequences lead to changes in voluntary behavior (Andover, 2013). The behavior is shaped using reinforcements or punishments. An animal or human learns from the consequences (reinforcer or punishment) of its own behavior.

American psychologist Edward Thorndike (1874–1949) developed the law of effect: through studying animal behavior, Thorndike found that if a behavior is followed by a desirable outcome the likelihood of engaging in that behavior again is greater.

B.F. Skinner (1904-1990) expanded on Thorndike’s ideas to develop a set of principles. Skinner’s famous experiment with rats in Skinner boxes showed that rats could learn to respond to stimuli in certain ways to earn food rewards and avoid electrical shocks (Stangor & Walinga, 2015).

In an Operant Conditioning context, Positive reinforcement strengthens a response by presenting something that is typically pleasant after the response, whereas negative reinforcement strengthens a response by reducing or removing something that is typically unpleasant.

Positive punishment weakens a response by presenting something typically unpleasant after the response, whereas negative punishment weakens a response by reducing or removing something that is typically pleasant.

Reinforcement and Punishment examples in a learning context:
  • Positive reinforcement: giving a student a prize after she gets the best grade on a test
  • Negative reinforcement: ignoring a student who shouts out answers but call on him when he raises his hand.
  • Positive punishment: giving a student extra homework after he misbehaves in class
  • Negative punishment: taking away a student’s tablet after she misbehave in class

Complex behaviours may be created through shaping, the process of guiding an organism’s behaviour to the desired outcome through the use of successive approximation to a final desired behaviour.

Behaviorism

In behaviorism, the organism is seen as “responding” to conditions (stimuli) set by the outer environment and by inner biological processes.

In the early 20th century, psychologist John B. Watson (1878–1958) expanded upon Pavlov’s findings. He stated that psychology should be studied through objective, observable behaviors rather than subjective, internal thoughts and consciousness. Experience and environment (rather than internal motivations or inherited traits) dictate who or what a person becomes (how he/she behaves).
